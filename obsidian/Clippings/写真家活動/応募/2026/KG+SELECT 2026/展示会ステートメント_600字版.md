# 展示会ステートメント「季節の翳り」（600字版）

## 日本語（596字）

「季節の翳り」は、日本美学における陰翳の感覚を現代に再解釈し、失われつつある季節の移ろいを体感させる試みである。プラチナプリントを和紙に焼き付け、額装から解放して空間に浮かべることで、写真を単なる「イメージの鑑賞物」から「光と時間を伴った体験」へと拡張する。

作品は天地を薄い檜板で挟み、ピアノ線で天井から吊り下げる。檜の木肌は和紙やプラチナの黒と調和し、障子や掛軸といった日本的意匠を想起させる。照明は各作品の上部に配置した電球色のスポット1灯のみとし、唯一の光源が闇の中で作品を浮かび上がらせる。それは孤月のように場を照らし、静謐で祈りに似た空気感を醸成する。

空間中央には、檜の台座の上に撮影に用いた花器を設置し、そこに茶花を活ける。会期中、二十四節気は「清明」「穀雨」「立夏」と移ろい、生花は週ごとに入れ替わる。写真が永遠に静止する「記録」であるのに対し、生花は現在を体現しながら朽ちていく「生」を示す。両者を対照することで、観者は「永続と無常」「記憶と現在」の二重性を目撃する。

和紙・檜・茶花という日本文化を象徴する素材を用いながらも、単なる伝統再現ではない。二十四節気という暦に沿った構成は、気候変動によって季節感が失われつつある現代への問いかけである。展示空間は、失われゆく季節への静かな挽歌であると同時に、未来への警告として機能する。時間の不可逆性と文化の記憶を問い直す。

## English

*Shadows of Seasons* takes as its starting point Jun'ichirō Tanizaki's meditation on shadow and light in *In Praise of Shadows* (1933), reimagining his aesthetics of darkness for an urgent contemporary context. Where Tanizaki celebrated the beauty found in dimness and patina, this exhibition addresses a starker reality: the loss of seasonal rhythms that have structured Japanese culture for over a millennium. By printing platinum images onto *washi* (Japanese handmade paper) and suspending them freely in space rather than framing them behind glass, the work transforms photography from an object of visual contemplation into an immersive experience of light, materiality, and temporal loss.

Each work is mounted between thin cypress boards and suspended from the ceiling with piano wire. The grain of the cypress resonates with the blackness of platinum and the texture of *washi*, evoking traditional Japanese spatial elements—*shoji* screens, hanging scrolls, paper lanterns. A single warm spotlight illuminates each piece from above, while the rest of the space remains in darkness. This solitary light source—like a lone moon—allows the works to emerge from shadow, creating an atmosphere of quietude and contemplation akin to prayer. The fibers of the *washi* catch the light, revealing subtle gradations of shadow that embody *in'ei* itself.

At the center of the space, a flower vessel used during the photographic sessions rests on a cypress pedestal, arranged with *chabana* (tea ceremony flowers). Throughout the exhibition period, the Twenty-Four Solar Terms shift from *Seimei* (Clear and Bright) through *Kokuu* (Grain Rain) to *Rikka* (Start of Summer), and the flowers are replaced weekly. While the photographs represent time arrested—permanent records in platinum's archival permanence—the fresh flowers embody living presence, withering and being renewed. This juxtaposition creates a temporal dialectic: viewers witness the duality of permanence and impermanence, memory and present moment.

Though the exhibition employs materials deeply rooted in Japanese culture—*washi*, cypress, tea flowers—it is not an exercise in traditionalism. The calendar structure of the Twenty-Four Solar Terms, once a precise framework for reading nature's rhythms, now poses urgent questions about a world where climate change has destabilized seasonal patterns. The exhibition space functions simultaneously as a quiet elegy for vanishing seasons and as a warning for the future. It asks us to reconsider the irreversibility of time and the fragility of cultural memory built upon natural cycles now in disarray.
