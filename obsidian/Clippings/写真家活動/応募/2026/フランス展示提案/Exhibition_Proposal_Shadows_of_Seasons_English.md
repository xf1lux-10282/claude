## 1. Exhibition Overview

### Title
**"Shadows of Seasons / 季節の翳り"**
Subtitle: Twenty-Four Solar Terms: Tea Flowers (二十四節気:茶花)

### Concept
This exhibition documents and expresses the loss of seasonal awareness caused by climate change through the lens of Japan's traditional time system, the Twenty-Four Solar Terms (Nijūshisekki), and through photographs of tea flowers. Reinterpreting the Japanese aesthetic explored in Jun'ichirō Tanizaki's *In Praise of Shadows* (1933), the work preserves fading seasonal memories using platinum/palladium printing, a technique dating from the 19th century.

### Exhibition Period
**Mid-November to Early December 2026 (2-4 weeks)**
Proposed to coincide with Paris Photo 2026 (typically held in November)

### Venue
Embassy of Japan in France or Maison de la culture du Japon à Paris (MCJP)

## 2. Background and Significance

### 2-1. The "Crisis of Seasonal Time" Due to Climate Change

The Twenty-Four Solar Terms originated in ancient China, dividing the solar year into 24 precise periods. In Japan, this system has formed the foundation of culture for over a millennium, informing the selection of tea flowers in tea ceremony, seasonal words (kigo) in haiku, and everyday seasonal awareness.

However, climate change has created a severe disconnect between the seasons indicated by the Twenty-Four Solar Terms and actual climatic phenomena.

**Tokyo Temperature Data: 100 Years of Change (Maximum Temperature)**:

| Solar Term | Period | 1925-26 | 1991-2020 Avg | 2025 | 100-Year Change |
|------------|--------|---------|---------------|------|-----------------|
| Start of Summer (立夏) | ~May 5 | 23.9℃ | 22.5℃ | 23.9℃ | ±0℃ |
| Lesser Fullness of Grain (小満) | ~May 21 | 22.3℃ | 24.2℃ | 24.7℃ | +2.4℃ |
| Grain in Ear (芒種) | ~Jun 6 | 21.3℃ | 25.3℃ | 28.8℃ | **+7.5℃** |
| Summer Solstice (夏至) | ~Jun 21 | 25.4℃ | 26.4℃ | 31.4℃ | **+6.0℃** |
| Lesser Heat (小暑) | ~Jul 7 | 30.3℃ | 28.9℃ | 35.6℃ | **+5.3℃** |
| Greater Heat (大暑) | ~Jul 23 | 30.4℃ | 30.6℃ | 34.3℃ | **+3.9℃** |
| Start of Autumn (立秋) | ~Aug 8 | 29.9℃ | 31.8℃ | 35.0℃ | **+5.1℃** |
| Limit of Heat (処暑) | ~Aug 23 | - | 30.9℃ | 35.8℃ | **+5℃+** |
| White Dew (白露) | ~Sep 8 | - | 29.1℃ | 35.0℃ | **+5℃+** |
| Autumnal Equinox (秋分) | ~Sep 23 | 25.6℃ | 25.8℃ | 26.2℃ | +0.6℃ |

*(Source: Japan Meteorological Agency)*

A century ago (1925-26), "Greater Heat" (the hottest period of the year) registered 30.4℃—a temperature that aligned with the seasonal sensibility the term evokes. "Grain in Ear" (around June 6, the time for sowing grains) was a mild 21.3℃.

However, in 2025, "Grain in Ear" reaches 28.8℃ (+7.5℃), "Summer Solstice" hits 31.4℃ (+6.0℃), and "Start of Autumn" reaches 35.0℃ (+5.1℃). Though "Start of Autumn" marks the calendar beginning of fall, 35℃ can no longer be called "autumn."

Over the span of a century, the seasonal sensibility indicated by the Twenty-Four Solar Terms has completely diverged from climatic reality. This is not mere "global warming" but a **qualitative collapse of seasons**.

### 2-2. Personal Origin: The Memory of Osmanthus

The artist was born in Kyoto in October. The fragrance of osmanthus blossoms at his family home would signal the approach of his birthday each year. After moving to Tokyo, this fragrance no longer arrives in October. As Octobers have grown hotter, the osmanthus scent now arrives late, signaling "the year's approaching end" rather than his birth.

This loss of a personal seasonal marker drives this project. Climate change manifests not as statistical data but as **the collapse of an intimate relationship with seasons built over a lifetime**, inscribed in our bodies.

### 2-3. Cultural and Academic Value

#### UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage
The Twenty-Four Solar Terms were inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2016. Yet the seasons this system describes no longer exist. This exhibition serves as both elegy and warning for this cultural and ecological loss.

#### Edo Painting Tradition: Sakai Hōitsu
Sakai Hōitsu (1761-1828), a painter of the late Edo period, depicted each of the twelve months through flowers and birds in his *Twelve Months of Flowers and Birds*. In *Plum Blossoms by Moonlight*, he captured the delicate beauty of plum blossoms illuminated by moonlight. This work's use of "moonlight-like illumination" for photographing tea flowers is a direct homage to Hōitsu's moonlight expressions. It carries forward the Japanese painting tradition of expressing seasons through flowers, using contemporary photographic techniques.

#### Contemporary Reinterpretation of Tanizaki's *In Praise of Shadows*
Jun'ichirō Tanizaki's 1933 essay *In Praise of Shadows* defended the Japanese aesthetic of "shadows" (kageri) against Western emphasis on brightness. This exhibition reinterprets these "shadows" as the fading "twilight" of disappearing seasons.

#### Platinum/Palladium Printing Technique
This precious technique dates from the 19th century. Its subtle tonal gradations and permanence make it the most appropriate medium for transmitting fading seasonal memories to the future.

## 3. About the Artwork

### 3-1. Creation Process
- Tea flowers photographed under moonlight-like illumination
- After photography, platinum/palladium prints hand-coated and processed
- Printed on handmade washi (Tosa Hakkin-shi)

### 3-2. Technical Specifications
- **Print Size**: 240mm × 363mm
  - Short edge 24cm = corresponds to the 24 solar terms
  - Long edge 363mm = approximates 365 days of the year
- **Technique**: Hand-coated platinum/palladium printing
- **Paper**: Handmade washi (Tosa Hakkin-shi)
- **Edition**: 8
  - Japanese auspicious number (suehirogari - "末広がり")
  - 8 on its side = ∞ (infinity, eternal cycle)

### 3-3. Number of Works
**20-24 pieces** (production in progress)
- Spring: 4-5 pieces
- Summer: 10-12 pieces (visually expressing seasonal expansion)
- Autumn: 2-3 pieces (expressing seasonal compression)
- Winter: 3-4 pieces

Currently completed: approximately 11 pieces

Works arranged by season, with density expressing seasonal distortion spatially.

- Start of Spring, Rain Water, Awakening of Insects, Spring Equinox, Clear and Bright, Grain Rain

- Start of Summer, Lesser Fullness of Grain, Grain in Ear, Summer Solstice, Lesser Heat, Greater Heat, Start of Autumn, Limit of Heat

**Autumn Zone** (sparse arrangement):
- White Dew, Autumnal Equinox
- Visual "instant"

**Winter Zone** (diffused presence):
- Cold Dew, Frost Descent, Start of Winter, Light Snow, Heavy Snow, Winter Solstice, Lesser Cold, Greater Cold

### Biography
Daisuke Kinoshita works with themes of natural transformation and temporal memory, creating photographs using the platinum/palladium printing process.

In childhood, the fragrance of osmanthus blossoms at his family home's entrance would signal the approach of his October birthday each year. Over time, however, that fragrance arrived later and later, eventually becoming a marker not of autumn's beginning but of year's end. This personal experience of seasonal distortion became the origin of his concern for natural rhythms disrupted by climate change.

After a career as a graphic designer, he transitioned to photography. While documenting landscapes following the 2011 Great East Japan Earthquake, he began exploring the relationship between time and memory. During the COVID-19 pandemic, increased time spent with family at the waterside led to a profound interest in water as subject matter, and he began addressing universal themes of change and transmission.

During this period, he committed deeply to platinum printing, a 19th-century process. He views this technique's rich tonal range and archival permanence as ideally suited to expressing the irreversibility of time. While photography arrests a moment eternally, nature continues its relentless transformation—this tension became central to his practice.

His representative series *BLUE MIND* explores humanity's relationship with water through figures gathered at the water's edge. *Ruten* (Flux) examines the circulation of water and the transformation of prayer through traditional Japanese water culture, centered on Gokonomiya Shrine in Fushimi, Kyoto. Building on these works, he is now engaged with the *Tsukiyukihana* (Moon, Snow, Flowers) series. Through the traditional framework of the Twenty-Four Solar Terms, he reexamines the beauty of vanishing seasons, questioning how climate change has distorted seasonal awareness and cultural memory.
