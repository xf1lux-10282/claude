# Present Yet Absent
## Production Essay (Submitted for Shiogama Photo Festival Portfolio Review)

**Daisuke Kinoshita**
**February 15, 2026**

---

## Preface — Confronting Loss

I had been photographing *chabana*—flowers arranged in the spirit of the tea ceremony—with the theme of seasonal distortion. But then I was confronted with a question that no technique or aesthetic could answer.

One day, without warning, I learned that my father had been given a terminal diagnosis. In that moment, the world changed. Or more precisely: the world itself did not change. What changed was the very foundation by which I measured it. Time, boundary, existence—everything I had taken as given began to tremble.

**What is existence? Where does boundary lie? What is time?**

These three questions became the origin of the triptych *Tsuki Yuki Hana* — Moon, Snow, Flower.

I grew up in Kyoto. There, the sense of the lunar calendar still lives within festivals and the tea ceremony, and measuring time by the phase of the moon was something my body learned before my mind did. The decision to name this triptych *Tsuki Yuki Hana*—Moon, Snow, Flowers—rather than the conventional *Yuki Tsuki Hana*—Snow, Moon, Flowers—was intentional. The moon comes first because in Kyoto, the moon was the very baseline by which the world was measured.

## Moon: Photographing the Trace of My Father

From the moment I heard his prognosis, I found myself reaching, without intention, toward memories of the time I had spent with him.

Yet what I could clearly recall was not the texture of those moments—what we had done together, or failed to do. What I spoke of, what expression he wore—the details grew vague, receding. But the fact of having been with him remained sharply present in my mind.

**This, I came to understand, is the contour of existence.**

One day, my daughter came down with a cold and asked me to grate an apple for her. In that instant, a memory of my grandmother surfaced. As I stood grating the apple, I tried to remember her. Was it a red apple or a green one? Her expression, her voice—all of it had faded. But the fact that, whenever I was unwell, she would always grate an apple for me—that remained vivid, untouched. As I held the apple and examined it, the hollow at the base of the stem—the *kōka*—looked to me like the craters of the moon.

The fact persists. The detail dissolves.

That day, I placed one of the apples I had bought for my daughter on the windowsill and photographed it in backlight. When I reviewed the image, the contour was sharp, but the face of the apple was submerged in shadow. Yet there was no doubt that it was *there*.

This was what I had been looking for. The contour of presence. The trace of what had been. It became, gradually, clear.

I decided to photograph this contour. I was not trying to photograph the memory of my father's face. I was photographing ***omokage*—the mode of existence that remembrance itself constitutes**.

Japanese has a word, *omokage*. It is not a memory of a face. It is the way in which someone *existed*. When a person dies, the precise details of their expression fade from memory. But *omokage* remains. This is how existence continues after death—as *omokage*. As something visible only within memory.

The moon does not emit its own light. It continues to exist in a position where the light no longer fully reaches it. It cannot be seen in complete light. It cannot be seen in complete darkness. Only when there is darkness, and within that darkness a faint light falls, can *omokage* be perceived. This series does not concern the moon itself.

It concerns **the condition of the moon**—present, yet closing itself to meaning the moment one tries to look.

I chose apples as my subject because of the story of Newton and gravity. What mattered was not that the apple fell—it was that an event everyone had witnessed suddenly *demanded to be read* at a particular moment. Crucially, I do not fix the variety. The first apple I photographed was *Fuji*. The next was *Kōgyoku*. The one after that, I did not check. The variety changes, but the fact of its being an apple does not. The moon appears differently each night—full, crescent, half. But it is never anything other than the moon.

It has long been said that one should view both the *jūgoya* moon—the full moon of the fifteenth night—and the *jūsanya* moon—the thirteenth night, the *nochi no tsukimi*, the "later moon-viewing." The *jūsanya* moon is slightly less than full, yet it carries a soft, rounded impression, a gentleness the perfect full moon cannot hold. I do not wish to look only at certain, legible proof of existence; I wish also to cherish what remains afterward as *omokage*—the trace that lingers. To hold dear those who leave their mark on the heart while still living—as one cherishes the later moon.

I chose platinum/palladium printing for its permanence. Platinum particles are absorbed deep into the paper's fibers. Unlike gelatin silver prints, platinum prints are chemically stable and do not change.

*Omokage*, too, does not change. The expression fades—but the *omokage* is eternal.

As something stable, substantial, unchanging. The five-hundred-year lifespan of a platinum print stands in contrast to my father's finite life. He will eventually be gone. But this print will remain for five hundred years. **The mode of existence that is *omokage*** will be encountered by someone's eyes five hundred years from now.

## Snow: Photographing What Persists Beneath the Cover

When I received my father's terminal diagnosis, I found myself in ongoing conflict—between the fact I had to accept and a self that refused to accept it.

Hokkaido was a place where memories of my father were concentrated. Among the few memories I hold clearly of my father, who was consumed by work, the strongest is Hokkaido, which we visited to ski from morning until night. And the last trip I took with my father was also to Hokkaido, in winter. From there, I found myself wanting to see snow.

There was another reason. Falling snow carried two contradictory images at once. After being confronted with the limit of a prognosis, there was the sensation of life slowly spilling away, flake by flake. But at the same time, from the fact that my father was still alive in this very moment, there was a feeling of life continuously welling up. The fallen snow accumulates and becomes a snowfield. That white accumulation looked to me like a gathering of life's fragments.

One winter, when a severe cold wave gripped all of Japan, I traveled to Hokkaido with my camera. In the deep midwinter snowfields, I thought, the snow will not melt. I believed I could escape from transience. But standing on the ground there, I realized: no matter how deep the snow, when spring comes, it will be gone. There was no escape.

The moment I looked at the snowy landscape through the viewfinder, something caught in my chest. The boundary between snowfield and sky was indistinct. In the unrelenting fall of snow, where did the earth end and the sky begin? But from that moment, what I saw was no longer a landscape. The line that should exist between heaven and earth—between near and far—had vanished. What disappeared was not the visibility of that line. It was the capacity for judgment itself.

From that moment, the question that had driven me changed. Not *where is the boundary*—but *does life not continue even after the boundary has gone*? When white snow meets white sky, distinction dissolves and the middle disappears. But beneath the snow, the buds wait in silence, and the roots continue. What was lost was not life itself—it was the visible marker by which we confirmed that life was there. Through being covered, existence is paradoxically proved.

For the Snow series, I deliberately chose gelatin silver printing. Snow is called *ginsekai* in Japanese—a world of silver. Gelatin silver prints, known in Japanese as *ginen purinto* (silver-salt prints), are, true to their name, a world of silver. I wanted to connect the earth buried beneath snow with a paper surface on which silver particles quietly receive light. I chose semi-gloss paper not for brilliant reflection, but to express the existence beneath the snow through a quietly abiding light.

## Flower: Photographing the Dissolving Season

Before welcoming guests, my mother always arranged a single flower—at the entrance, in the *tokonoma* alcove. Not an elaborate arrangement; she would simply place the flower that was blooming at that precise moment in the season. This was the spirit of *chabana*—using a flower to make the current season present in the space. As a child, I never thought deeply about its meaning. But later I understood: what made that act possible was a sure correspondence between the flower and the season. Because this flower is blooming, it is spring. Because that fragrance is in the air, autumn has arrived. Flowers were a language that announced the season. That language is quietly ceasing to function.

I was born in October. Each year at my childhood home, the fragrance of *kinmokusei*—osmanthus—announced the arrival of my birthday. As a child, in the days approaching my birthday, I would check daily for the osmanthus to bloom. Wondering, *not yet?* And then one day, the fragrance would suddenly arrive. *Ah, my birthday is coming.* This was how the season announced itself to me.

But over time, something began to unsettle me. October 2024. My birthday came, but the fragrance did not. It arrived in November instead. No longer heralding my birthday, it now hinted at the approach of year's end. In that moment, I experienced a sense of loss for which I had no words. What I had lost was not the flower. **It was the way of measuring time itself—a framework I had built across a lifetime through the language of flowers.**

When I lost this deeply personal temporal marker, I understood in my body what climate change truly means. One day, I came across a news report: the *nijūshisekki*—the traditional East Asian calendar of twenty-four solar terms—had been registered as a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. This was in 2016. A millennium-old calendar was being "protected" as cultural heritage, while the actual seasons were collapsing. **What happens when flowers can no longer tell us which season it is?**

The flowers in this series are photographed at night. At first, it was not intentional. One night, on a walk, I saw a flower illuminated by a streetlight. I could not identify it. Cherry blossom? Plum? In that moment, I decided. I would photograph at night. I would illuminate with restrained light evoking moonlight. I would deliberately withhold species, name, and seasonal signs. When recognition fails, the act of perception itself becomes the subject.

While working on the Flower series, I returned to Zeami's *Fūshikaden*: *"To conceal is to be the flower; to not conceal is not to be the flower."* Beauty arises in the viewer's heart precisely because not everything is shown. But the question I am asking is not about the beauty created by an artist's deliberate concealment. It is about the reality that **the flower has come to be in a "concealed" state as a consequence of the loss of seasonal context—the temporal framework itself**. Photographing at night, illuminating with restrained moonlike light, withholding species, name, and seasonal sign—this is not merely visual shadow. It is an attempt to layer onto a single surface the epistemological shadow of no longer being able to read the season from a flower. You look at the flower and ask, *what is this?* That question becomes: *what month is it?* That question becomes: *does season still exist?*

For the Flower series, I also chose platinum/palladium printing. The five-hundred-year lifespan of a platinum print is a form of resistance against a dissolving cultural understanding. The framework of seasons will collapse. But this work will remain for five hundred years. Someone will look at this print five centuries from now. Will the twenty-four solar terms still exist then? Will the culture of reading seasons through flowers still be alive? The answer, in all likelihood, is no. But this print will remain. As evidence of a lost sense of time.

> **[Technical Note] The Meaning of Materiality**
>
> *Platinum/Palladium Print (Moon, Flower)*
>
> Three years working with platinum printing. Many failures. But with each failure, my understanding of platinum's materiality deepened. Platinum particles are absorbed deep into the paper's fibers. Unlike gelatin silver prints, which remain on the surface, platinum becomes one with the paper. With each platinum print I made, I was drawn increasingly to its permanence. This print will outlast me. It will outlast my father. It will survive five hundred years. **The permanence of matter proves the transience of existence.**
>
> *Gelatin Silver Print (Snow)*
>
> When I chose gelatin silver printing for the Snow series, I was sometimes asked: "Why not keep it consistent? For a triptych, shouldn't you use the same process throughout?" But for me, the choice of process carries the core meaning of the work. Snow is called *ginsekai* in Japanese—a world of silver. Gelatin silver prints are, true to their name, a world of silver. I wanted to connect the covered earth with a paper surface on which silver particles quietly receive light. I chose semi-gloss paper not for brilliant reflection, but to express, through a quietly abiding light, the existence that persists beneath the snow.
>
> *The Aesthetic of Jun'ichirō Tanizaki*
>
> This contrast between processes came to me while reading Tanizaki's *In'ei Raisan* (*In Praise of Shadows*, 1933): *"Beauty exists not in the object itself, but in the patterns of shadow and light that objects create."* The immutability of platinum alongside the luminosity of silver. Permanence and transience. This contrast is what reveals the modes of existence.

## Closing — That Which Illuminates Late

Through this triptych, what am I photographing?

Not the *omokage* of my father. Not a snowy landscape. Not flowers.

I am photographing **the modes of existence**.

How existence has come to be in a way it could only be. I am attempting to touch those modes with care.

This is neither accusation nor documentation. Like the moon viewed on an autumn evening—*nochi no tsukimi*, the "later moon-viewing"—it illuminates, belatedly, a world already trembling.

In February 2026, my father is fighting with everything he has to live. But the moment that will inevitably come is not a distant prospect.

The seasons are in collapse. Yet the flowers continue to bloom.

The boundaries are dissolving. Yet the snow continues to fall.

The traces are fading. Yet existence continues.

This work does not confront the end. It confronts **what continues to persist within the end**.

**Daisuke Kinoshita**
