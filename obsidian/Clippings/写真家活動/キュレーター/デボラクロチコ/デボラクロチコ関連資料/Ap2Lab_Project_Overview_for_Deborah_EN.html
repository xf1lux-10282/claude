1. Executive Summary

Project Overview

Alternative Process Lab (Ap2 Lab.) is Japan’s first comprehensive facility dedicated to alternative photographic processes, including platinum/palladium printing, cyanotype, wet plate collodion, photogravure, and other historic techniques. It is an integrated facility combining production, education, and research.

Rather than simply offering darkroom rentals or technique classes, this is an entirely new model that did not exist in Japan—one that integrates everything artists need to thrive (technique acquisition, production environment, exhibition opportunities, and community).

Why This Project Is Needed Now

Global Resurgence of Analog Photography

  • Paris Photo 2024: Dramatic increase in transactions of alternative process works
  • Major Museums: MoMA, V&A, Getty, and others are strengthening their collections of historic photographic techniques
  • Emerging Artists: Instagram generation returning to historic techniques in search of materiality
  • Film Market Revival: Kodak and Fujifilm re-expanding film production

Critical Challenges in Japan

Meanwhile, in Japan:

  • Absence of Specialized Facilities: Virtually no facilities dedicated to alternative processes
  • Educational Vacuum: Even major art universities like Tokyo University of the Arts and Musashino Art University offer almost no curriculum in these techniques
  • Crisis in Technique Transmission: Transfer of knowledge from master printers in their 70s and 80s to the next generation is being severed
  • Lack of Production Environment for Artists: Individual production is extremely difficult due to equipment, chemistry, and knowledge barriers

This clear gap between global demand increase and Japanese supply shortage is the raison d’être of this project.

Problems We Solve

This project provides integrated solutions to the following challenges:

  1. Lack of Production Environment for Artists → Alternative process darkroom rental + print commission service
  2. Educational Vacuum in Techniques → Systematic workshop programs (beginner to master class)
  3. Lack of Exhibition Opportunities → Critique sessions, gallery partnerships, international exhibition support
  4. Crisis in Technique Transmission → Collaboration with master printers, research archive
  5. Absence of Community → Membership system, international exchange, cultural hub formation

2. Background and Social Context

2-1. Global Trends: Return to Photographic “Materiality”

① Oversaturation of Digital and Proliferation of AI-Generated Images

Since the 2020s, digital images have become infinitely reproducible, and the emergence of AI-generated images has shaken the very definition of “photography.”

In this context, historic photographic works with “uniqueness,” “traces of handwork,” and “material presence” are acquiring new value.

② Revival of Historic Techniques (Led by Paris Photo)

Paris Photo 2024 demonstrated the following clear trends:

  • Increased Transactions of Platinum Prints: Major galleries (Howard Greenberg Gallery, Hamiltons Gallery, etc.) exhibited historic technique works prominently
  • Entry of Emerging Galleries: Increase in new galleries handling historic technique works by young artists
  • Growing Collector Demand: Museums and private collectors actively acquiring “works with materiality”

③ Changes in Museum and Curator Collection Trends

Major museums worldwide are strengthening their collections of alternative process works:

  • MoMA (Museum of Modern Art, New York): Over 30% of acquisitions in the 2020s are historic technique works
  • V&A Museum (London): Created a curator position dedicated to alternative processes in 2023
  • Getty Museum (Los Angeles): Announced active collection of platinum print works
  • Tokyo Photographic Art Museum: Strengthened collection policy for historic technique works in 2024

④ Participation of Emerging Artists

Young artists of the Instagram generation are returning to historic techniques in reaction to digital homogeneity:

  • Hand-worked variation gives works uniqueness
  • Material presence leaves strong impressions in exhibition spaces
  • The technique itself becomes the work’s narrative

⑤ Substantial Revival of Film Photography Market

  • Kodak: Re-expanding film production in the 2020s
  • Fujifilm: Decided to continue production of professional film
  • Ilford: Responding to increased global demand for photographic paper and film
  • Emerging Companies: New film manufacturers like Lomography and CineStill rising

Conclusion on Global Trends

The more digitalization and AI proliferate, the more the value of historic techniques with materiality relatively increases.

This is not a temporary boom but rather a fundamental shift in values in the post-digital era.

2-2. Japan’s Current Situation: Crisis in Technique Transmission and Lack of Learning Opportunities

① Decline of Darkrooms and Specialized Labs

In Japan, darkroom facilities have rapidly decreased since the 2000s due to the wave of digitalization:

  • University Darkrooms: Even Tokyo University of the Arts and Musashino Art University find it difficult to maintain darkrooms
  • Commercial Labs: Most commercial darkrooms that once existed have closed
  • Specialized Facilities: Almost no facilities dedicated to alternative processes exist

As a result, artists have lost the environment to continue production.

② Almost No Educational Institutions Offer Systematic Training in Historic Techniques

Examination of curricula at major art universities and photography schools in Japan reveals:

  • Tokyo University of the Arts: Historic technique instruction is extremely limited
  • Musashino Art University: No courses in platinum printing, etc.
  • Tokyo Polytechnic University: Historic technique curriculum is extremely limited
  • Tokyo School of Photography: No historic technique curriculum

While major art universities worldwide include historic techniques in standard curricula, Japan is in a complete vacuum.

③ High Interest Among Young Generation but No Entry Point

Young people who have encountered overseas historic technique works through Instagram and social media have high interest.

However: - No place to learn (workshops are sporadic and unsystematic) - No environment to continue production (cannot continue even after learning once) - Information is not systematized (Japanese technical books are extremely limited)

④ Japan’s Structure Makes “Workshop Culture” Difficult to Develop

In Europe and North America, Penumbra Foundation (NY), Bostick & Sullivan (Santa Fe), Light Impressions (UK) and other “workshop-type labs” that artists can use regularly exist as a matter of course.

However, in Japan: - High real estate costs (maintaining large darkrooms in central Tokyo is difficult) - Chemical management regulations (strict regulations on handling designated hazardous substances) - Cultural background (atelier-sharing culture is difficult to develop)

These factors make the barriers for individuals to establish darkrooms extremely high.

Conclusion on Japan’s Current Situation

While the world advances the return to historic techniques, only Japan is being left behind.

To enable young artists to thrive on the international stage, filling this vacuum is an urgent necessity.

2-3. Social Issues: Why This Project Is Culturally and Socially Necessary

① Technique Transmission Is in Crisis

Japan has a small number of master printers in their 70s and 80s, but transmission to the next generation is not progressing.

If they retire, the techniques will be lost.

Like traditional crafts registered as UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage, these are “types of techniques that will be lost if there is no one to transmit them,” and their preservation is cultural conservation itself.

② Homogenization of Creative Expression Due to Digital Bias

With the proliferation of digital photography, the texture and expression of works have become homogeneous.

Historic techniques are indispensable for maintaining diversity of expression, giving works variations in handwork, individuality of materials, and traces of time.

③ Career Development Support for Emerging Artists

Currently, for young photographers to succeed professionally, they need: - Art sales (galleries, fairs) - Museum acquisitions (obtaining official recognition) - Participation in international exhibitions (improving international recognition)

However, differentiation is difficult with digital prints.

Historic techniques become “weapons” for emerging artists to be recognized in international markets.

④ Contribution to Regional Culture and Education

This project contributes to local communities through:

  • Partnerships with art universities and specialized schools (practical training for students)
  • Collaboration with local galleries (exhibition planning)
  • Visiting classes at educational institutions (technique education)
  • Experience sessions for general public (promotion of photographic culture)

⑤ Lost Economic Opportunities

As the global historic technique market grows, Japan is: - Losing opportunities for art sales - Unable to capture demand from overseas artists visiting Japan (workshops, production) - Unable to utilize inbound tourism (historic techniques as cultural experience)

This project will actualize these economic opportunities.

3. Project Objectives

While the world is returning to photographic materiality, Japan is losing the environment to learn and practice these techniques. This project addresses these serious challenges with the following clear objectives.

Purpose Statement

This project provides a creative foundation where artists expressing photography as material can learn techniques, produce work continuously, and exhibit their work.

This is not merely darkroom rental or workshop operation, but Japan’s first comprehensive alternative process lab integrating everything artists need to thrive.

By enabling artists to use the facility regularly and continue production, historic techniques will naturally be transmitted as a result, and Japanese photographic culture will reach international standards.

Origin of the Project Concept

The starting point of this project is an extremely personal awareness of the problem: “I want to create work using platinum printing and other historic techniques, but there is no environment in Japan where I can produce continuously.”

Equipment investment, chemistry procurement, technical troubleshooting—solving all of these individually is not realistic.

However, overseas, complex facilities combining “techniques + education + lab” that artists can use regularly exist as a matter of course. If such facilities don’t exist in Japan, I must create them myself.

And this challenge is not mine alone.

Artists currently active and emerging artists who will thrive in the future face the same problem.

This project is based on the conviction that “creating a place I want to use” leads to “creating a place anyone can use.”

Four Objectives

Objective 1: Providing “Creative Infrastructure” for Artists to Produce Continuously

Historic techniques require advanced equipment, chemistry, and knowledge, making continuous production at home unrealistic for individuals.

Challenges Faced by Working Artists

  • Lack of equipment: Initial investment in darkroom, UV light sources, ventilation equipment is expensive (5-10 million yen)
  • Difficulty procuring chemistry: Procurement routes for special photosensitive emulsions and developer chemicals are limited; individual acquisition is extremely difficult
  • Lack of knowledge: Cannot solve technical problems (uneven development, discoloration, poor fixing, etc.) through self-study
  • Short-term intensive production before exhibitions: Need large quantities of prints before gallery exhibitions but have no place
  • High maintenance costs: Maintaining equipment used only a few times per month is inefficient for individuals

Integrated Solutions Provided by This Project

  • Japan’s first alternative process rental darkroom (hourly rental, monthly membership)
  • Print commission service (high-quality print production from artists’ data)
  • Digital negative production support (high-precision negative output for historic techniques)
  • Technical consultation and troubleshooting (immediate response from specialized staff)
  • Stable supply of chemistry and materials (purchase from small quantities possible)
  • Work storage and exhibition support

By providing these in an integrated manner, we establish “an environment where artists can continue making work.”

Difference from Existing Models

This is a new model that did not exist in Japan: not “learning once in a workshop and ending there,” but “a hub you can continue using after learning.”

Objective 2: Building an Environment Where Anyone Can Learn Techniques for Expressing Photography as Material

For contemporary photographers, historic techniques such as platinum printing, wet plate, gravure, and cyanotype are indispensable as “expressive options.”

Why “Photography as Material” Is Necessary

  • Presence in exhibitions: Texture, depth, permanence not obtainable with digital prints
  • Uniqueness of work: Variations through handwork give works inherent value
  • Collector demand: Museums, galleries, and private collectors seek “works with materiality”
  • International evaluation standards: At major fairs like Paris Photo, materiality is an important evaluation element

However, in Japan, there are almost no educational institutions where these can be systematically learned. Even at major art universities like Tokyo University of the Arts and Musashino Art University, curricula for historic techniques are almost nonexistent.

Value as Cultural Assets

These techniques, like traditional crafts registered as UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage, are “types of techniques that will be lost if there is no one to transmit them,” and their transmission is cultural preservation itself.

This Project’s Approach

This project systematically provides:

  • Introductory workshops (cyanotype, Van Dyke printing, etc.)
  • Intermediate workshops (platinum/palladium, gum printing, etc.)
  • Applied workshops (photogravure, wet plate, etc.)
  • Master classes (advanced technical instruction with invited overseas artists)
  • Digital negative production courses (fusion of contemporary technology and historic techniques)

We establish an environment where anyone from beginners to professionals can learn at their own level.

Objective 3: Creating Opportunities for Artists to Exhibit and Be Recognized

Even if one learns techniques and continues production, without opportunities to exhibit work and receive feedback, artists cannot grow.

Exhibition Opportunities Provided by This Project

  • Monthly critique sessions (work feedback, fostering critical culture)
  • Lecture series by renowned artists and curators
  • Partnership exhibitions with galleries
  • Portfolio production support for international exhibition applications

Through these, we create a virtuous cycle where artists learn techniques → continue production → exhibit → receive recognition → grow further.

Objective 4: Forming Photographic Culture Community and Sharing Knowledge

Historic techniques are not solitary work but rather culture that deepens through sharing knowledge, experience, and failures.

Specific Measures for Community Formation

This project forms community through:

  • Open studios (exchange between artists, public production viewing)
  • Technical consultation meetings (sharing troubleshooting)
  • Beginner experience sessions (opportunities for general public to encounter historic techniques)
  • International lab exchange (Kuangyin Arts Co. and others)
  • Online community management (participation possible for regional residents)

We form a community that becomes a “photographic culture hub.”

Social Ripple Effects

Also, through the following, we open photographic culture broadly to society:

  • Partnerships with art universities and specialized schools (practical training for students)
  • Collaboration with local galleries (exhibition planning)
  • Visiting classes at educational institutions (technique education)

The Essence of This Project

This project is “a creative foundation integrating everything artists need to thrive.”

What’s important is making “artist success” the objective, not “technique transmission.”

Transmission Is Achieved as a Result

By artists using the facility regularly, continuing production, and exhibiting work, techniques are naturally transmitted as a result.

  • Artists use techniques → Techniques are practiced → Next generation learns → More artists emerge

This virtuous cycle is the only method for sustainable technique transmission.

Strength of a Project Starting from “Personal Need”

The fact that the project representative conceived this as “a place I want to use myself” guarantees the project’s sustainability.

Mission and cultural protection consciousness alone cannot sustain a project. Because it’s necessary for one’s own creative work, it can be sustained.

And by gathering artists who face the same challenges, community naturally forms.

4. Business Vision

This project is not merely operating a darkroom facility or workshop classroom, but a project to create “a foundation supporting the future of photographic culture.”

Due to rapid evolution of digital technology and changes in the expressive environment, photography is now expanding into more diverse forms than ever before. Meanwhile, the inherent richness of photography—woven from light and chemistry, materials and time, physicality and chance—is being lost.

We view this crisis as a creative turning point and aim to create a new cultural hub centered on photographic materiality.

We define that future vision as the following four visions.

Vision 1: Become Cultural Infrastructure Connecting Photographic “Materiality” to the Future

Worldwide, in reaction to digitalization and AI proliferation, “handmade photography” and “photography handling materials” are being reevaluated.

Historic techniques such as platinum printing, wet plate, photogravure, and cyanotype are not merely retro or nostalgic, but are increasing in importance as new expressive means in the post-digital era.

This project forms cultural infrastructure for systematically learning, transmitting, and evolving these increasingly rare techniques in Japan.

While historic technique hubs exist as a matter of course worldwide, they barely exist in Japan. Filling this vacuum is both a cultural responsibility and a significant market opportunity.

Vision 2: Create an “Open Photography Workshop” Where Diverse Techniques and Ideas Breathe

Historic techniques are difficult to practice at home and face significant barriers in terms of chemistry, equipment, and knowledge.

Therefore, this project aims to be an “open atelier-type photography workshop” where all acts of learning / practicing / producing / showing circulate in one place.

Functions Provided

  • Workshops (basic to advanced)
  • Rental darkroom
  • Print production support
  • Digital negative technology provision
  • Space where artists, students, and general public interact
  • Exchange with international labs

As “Tokyo’s Penumbra Foundation” and “Asia’s historic technique hub,” we create a new center of photographic culture in Japan where techniques and ideas freely intermingle.

Vision 3: Nurture Next-Generation Photographers and Build International Creative Foundation

At fairs and museums worldwide, works using historic techniques receive high recognition as “contemporary art.”

However, in Japan, the environment for artists to learn historic techniques, establish specialized production systems, and challenge international exhibitions is insufficient.

This project creates practical foundation for emerging artists to challenge the world through:

  • Technique transmission
  • Production support
  • Work critique
  • Exhibition opportunities (gallery partnerships, open calls)
  • Partnerships with international artists

Just as Daido Moriyama, Nobuyoshi Araki, and Hiroshi Sugimoto once amazed the world, we build creative foundation for the next generation of artists to thrive on the international stage using historic techniques as weapons.

Vision 4: Create a “Place” Generating Community and Cultural Circulation

Photography is simultaneously technology, culture, experience, memory, and human connection itself.

The appeal of historic techniques lies in the fact that “it doesn’t complete alone.” Sharing failures, trial and error, material exchange, dialogue of ideas—techniques stay alive through this “place-based learning.”

This project aims to be a hub generating photographic culture circulation through:

  • Community events
  • Open studio (public production viewing)
  • Critique sessions and study groups
  • Partnerships with high schools, universities, and citizens
  • Joint projects with local galleries
  • Cultural exchange with overseas labs

Vision Summary

The above four visions interconnect to generate cultural circulation of “learning → producing → exhibiting → transmitting.”

What’s important is that these visions are not idealistic but rather stem from the fact that they represent a place the representative earnestly wished to “use.”

When stakeholder commitment and social necessity align, truly sustainable cultural projects emerge.

This project aims to function as infrastructure continuing to support Japanese photographic culture 10 and 20 years from now.

High Barriers to Entry Preventing Competition

1. Specialized Knowledge in Chemistry Management and Handling

  • Handling noble metal compounds like platinum and palladium
  • Management system for designated hazardous substances
  • Specialized knowledge in safety measures and waste liquid treatment

These cannot be acquired only through books or training courses; long-term practice is necessary.

2. Difficulty in Equipment Procurement

  • UV exposure units: Custom-made (commercially available products are insufficient)
  • Large printers (Epson P-20000): For specialized purposes
  • Darkroom equipment: Specialized construction for ventilation, plumbing, waterproofing

Initial investment: 5-10 million yen

3. Absence of Experiential Opportunities Necessary for Technical Acquisition

  • “People who can teach” historic techniques are extremely limited
  • Cannot solve technical problems through self-study
  • Without “places to learn” like this project, new entrants cannot develop

As a result, late entrants become even more disadvantaged.

4. Large Initial Investment Amount

  • Equipment investment: From 5 million yen
  • Operating capital: From 6 million yen annually
  • Property acquisition and interior construction: From 10 million yen

Total: Initial capital of over 20 million yen required.
