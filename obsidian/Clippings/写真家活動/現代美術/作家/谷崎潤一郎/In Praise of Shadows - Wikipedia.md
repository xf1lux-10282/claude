***In Praise of Shadows*** (陰翳礼讃, *In'ei Raisan*) is a 1933 essay on [Japanese aesthetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_aesthetics "Japanese aesthetics") by the Japanese author [Jun'ichirō Tanizaki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jun%27ichir%C5%8D_Tanizaki "Jun'ichirō Tanizaki"). It was translated into English, in 1977, by the academic students of [Japanese literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_literature "Japanese literature") Thomas J. Harper and [Edward Seidensticker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Seidensticker "Edward Seidensticker"). A new translation by Gregory Starr was published in 2017; a further translation, along with three additional essays, is slated to be published by [Tuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuttle_Publishing "Tuttle Publishing") in the fall of 2025.[^2]

## Publication history

Already sketched out in a series of comments that appear in *[Some Prefer Nettles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Some_Prefer_Nettles "Some Prefer Nettles")*, Tanizaki's aesthetic credo, in the more finished form of this essay, was originally published in 1933 in [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "Japanese language").

The English translation was published in 1977 by Leete's Island Books.[^1] The translation contains a foreword by architect and educator [Charles Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Willard_Moore "Charles Willard Moore") and an afterword by one of the translators, Thomas J. Harper. Harper was Senior Lecturer in [Japanese Literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_literature "Japanese literature") at the [Australian National University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian_National_University "Australian National University") in [Canberra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canberra "Canberra"). The other translator, [Edward Seidensticker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Seidensticker "Edward Seidensticker"), was Professor of Japanese Literature at [Columbia University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_University "Columbia University").[^3]

Much shorter than the author's novels, this book is a small meditative work of 73 pages, of which 59 are the essay itself.

In 2001, [Random House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_House "Random House") published a reprint in paperback.[^4]

A new English translation by Gregory Starr, with illustrations and photographs by Andrew Pothecary, was published by Sora Books in December 2017. The 102-page edition also includes a foreword by the Japanese architect [Kengo Kuma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kengo_Kuma "Kengo Kuma") and an afterword by Japanese literature Professor Eve Zimmerman of Wellesley College.[^5]

## Themes

The essay consists of 16 sections that discuss traditional [Japanese aesthetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_aesthetics "Japanese aesthetics") in contrast with change. Comparisons of light with darkness are used to contrast [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_culture "Western culture") and [Asian cultures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Asia "Culture of Asia"). The West, in its striving for progress, is presented as continuously searching for light and clarity, while the subtle and subdued forms of East Asian art and literature are seen by Tanizaki to represent an appreciation of shadow and subtlety, closely relating to the traditional Japanese concept of [*sabi*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wabi-sabi "Wabi-sabi"). In addition to contrasting light and dark, Tanizaki further considers the layered tones of various kinds of shadows and their power to reflect low sheen materials like gold embroidery, patina and cloudy crystals. In addition, he distinguishes between the values of gleam and shine.

The text presents personal reflections on topics as diverse as [architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architecture "Architecture") and its fittings, crafts, finishes, [jade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jade "Jade"), food, [cosmetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmetics "Cosmetics") and *[mono no aware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mono_no_aware "Mono no aware")* (the art of impermanence). Tanizaki explores in close description the use of space in buildings, [lacquerware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lacquerware "Lacquerware") by candlelight,[^1] monastery toilets [^6] and women in the dark of a brothel. The essay acts as "a classic description of the collision between the shadows of traditional Japanese interiors and the dazzling light of the modern age".[^7]

In the Sora Books edition, the 16 section themes are:

- On construction
- The toilet aesthetic
- A different course
- A novelist's daydreams
- On paper, tin and dirt
- Candlelight and lacquerware
- Bowls of broth
- The enigma of shadows
- An uncanny silence
- Reflections in darkness
- Shadows on the stage
- The woman of old
- Beauty in the dark
- A world of shadows
- A cool breeze in total darkness
- Final grumblings

## Cultural notes

Tanizaki's observations include cultural notes on topics such as arts and crafts, paper making, lacquerware design and the Japanese room. He gives a recipe for the unusual dish of [Persimmon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persimmon "Persimmon") leaf [Sushi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sushi "Sushi") on pages 60 to 62. He also refers to many historic places and temples, as well as celebrated eateries of the day, along with customs like "moon-viewing" (*[tsukimi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tsukimi "Tsukimi")*).

## Featured individuals

Among the historic and contemporary individuals mentioned in the essay are:[^8]

- [Natsume Sōseki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natsume_S%C5%8Dseki "Natsume Sōseki"), novelist, on the experience of textures and landscape via the traditional Japanese toilet, p. 9; Sōseki's *Pillow of Grass*, p. 26.
- [Saitō Ryokuu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sait%C5%8D_Ryokuu "Saitō Ryokuu"), poet quoted as saying "elegance is frigid", p. 10.
- [Buddha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddha "Buddha") and [Confucius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucius "Confucius"), p. 16.
- Kongō Iwao, Nō actor, p. 39.
- Baikō, aging Kabuki actor, p. 43.
- Takebaya Sanehiko, president of [Kaizō Publishing House](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaiz%C5%8D_\(magazine\) "Kaizō (magazine)") (which had published Tanizaki's earlier novel *[Quicksand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quicksand_\(Tanizaki_novel\) "Quicksand (Tanizaki novel)")*), p. 54.
- [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein"), scientist, p. 54.

## Reception

The work has been praised for its insight and relevance into issues of modernity and culture,[^9] and Tanizaki has been called an "ecological prophet".[^9] [A. C. Grayling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._C._Grayling "A. C. Grayling") has described Tanizaki's essay on Japanese taste as a "hymn to nuance" and an exercise in mindfulness.[^1]

> Junichiro Tanizaki selects for praise all things delicate and nuanced, everything softened by shadows and the patina of age, anything understated and natural—as for example the patterns of grain in old wood, the sound of rain dripping from eaves and leaves, or washing over the footing of a stone lantern in a garden, and refreshing the moss that grows about it – and by doing so he suggests an attitude of appreciation and mindfulness, especially mindfulness of beauty, as central to life lived well.[^1]

In the spirit of Tanizaki juxtaposing the cultures of east and west, Grayling notes a link to a similar approach and emphasis in the British writer [Walter Pater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Pater "Walter Pater") whose final essay about the Renaissance he quotes, "The service of speculative culture towards the human spirit is to rouse, to startle it to a life of constant and eager observation". Grayling concludes that the difference between the two essayists lies in the "tranquility" of Tanizaki and the "intensity" of Pater.[^1]

Regarding the new translation published by Sora Books, , author of *[Cloud Atlas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_Atlas_\(novel\) "Cloud Atlas (novel)")*, wrote, "A rhapsodic meditation on a vanishing world, its aesthetics and its values. Gregory Starr's new translation is pitch perfect and transparent." Stephen Mansfield, in the *Japan Times* wrote, "Tanizaki, in Gregory Starr’s new and highly accomplished translation, samples a number of instances where the use and perception of light differs from the West, noting that, where Western paper reflects light, traditional Japanese paper absorbs it." [^10]

The book also served as the primary inspiration for an album of the same name by musician Puma Blue.[^11]
