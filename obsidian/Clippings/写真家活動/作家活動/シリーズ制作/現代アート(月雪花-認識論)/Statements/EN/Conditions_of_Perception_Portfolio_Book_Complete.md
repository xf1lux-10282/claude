# Conditions of Perception
## Portfolio Book Complete Edition

**Daisuke Kinoshita**
**March 1, 2026**

---

I materialize photographs.

The image dwells in the substrate and exists as matter.

Perception becomes double.
Seeing the world and feeling the conditions of perception occur simultaneously.

Visibility is always partial.
Judgment depends on boundaries, and those boundaries waver.
Meaning is not fixed.

These are not defects of perception—they are the conditions of perception, and we see the world through the conditions of perception.

This work addresses not the subject itself, but the process through which perception emerges between subject and viewer.

The image is not a fixed answer.
It functions as a catalyst that brings forth the structures of sensitivity and memory latent within the viewer.

## Artist Essay

The world is not certain in itself. It appears certain through the conditions of perception.

When my father was diagnosed with cancer, the world did not change. What shifted were the conditions through which I understood it—time, boundary, presence. They no longer functioned as before.

This experience was personal, yet it reflected a broader instability. Information proliferates beyond measure. Judgment accelerates. Seasons fracture. The conditions that once stabilized our understanding of the world are quietly beginning to unravel.

This project does not address existence itself. It addresses the conditions through which existence becomes certain: visibility, judgment, and meaning.

The contemporary moment demands complete visibility and immediate understanding. However, perception does not function this way. Visibility is always partial. Judgment depends on boundaries, and those boundaries waver. Meaning is not fixed. These are not defects of perception. They are the conditions of perception.

This project seeks to reopen, through the materiality of photography, the depth of perception that arises through shadow, empty space, and concealment—in response to a contemporary culture that demands visibility and immediate understanding. Precisely because the conditions of perception waver, viewers reconnect with the image through their own memory, emotion, and experience. Wavering is not a defect. It is the generative condition through which the viewer's sensitivity operates.

Moon addresses the condition of visibility. Seeing is not the bringing of everything into light. The image cannot be formed without darkness, and emerges only partially, containing what remains unseen. Visibility is not the complete grasp of the object, but the condition for receiving it as something that remains uncertain.

I grew up in Kyoto, where the lunar calendar shaped the rhythm of life. The waxing and waning of the moon inscribed time into the body—a framework through which time became visible. From seasonal festivals to daily life, the lunar cycle remained a living cultural practice. Even in an ancient capital, those who consciously maintain this cultural rhythm are quietly diminishing, yet among those who preserve it intentionally, the practice continues.

When I think of my father, I encounter a strange sensation. Details blur. His face lacks clear definition. Yet the sense that he is there remains certain. One day, while grating an apple for my daughter, I traced the memory of my grandmother. I could recall neither color nor form. Yet the structure of the event—"my grandmother grated apples for me"—persisted.

I placed an apple by the window and photographed it in backlight. The contour emerged, the center withdrew into shadow. Its presence was undeniable. Yet the whole remained unseen. The moon does not emit its own light. It cannot be seen under total illumination. Nor can it be seen in complete darkness. It arises faintly only within the relation of light and shadow.

Visibility arises through the relation between light and darkness. What is seen is supported by what is unseen. What appears is always partial. What we see is never the whole. It is always partial. Visibility is not given. It is constructed.

In platinum/palladium printing, the image sinks into the interior of the paper. Light does not remain on the surface; it carries depth. This parallels how presence is felt—somewhere between what is seen and what persists.

Platinum/palladium printing requires time and labor. Yet its durability extends for centuries. This is not merely a preservation technique. It is an artist's responsibility to leave the conditions of perception felt in this era in a form that remains accessible to future generations. Digital images are disseminated instantly, consumed instantly, and forgotten instantly. However, the inquiry into the conditions of perception is not a temporary trend. It is a fundamental question that must continue to be asked as long as humans exist. To leave this inquiry as matter is to open the possibility of dialogue with the future. Creating each print by hand is not resistance to mechanical reproducibility. It is the materialization of perception through the viewer's bodily experience. Viewers "touch the work with their eyes." Taking time to see, changing angles and shifting position, sensing the slight fluctuation of the image—this experience itself exposes the conditions of perception.

Snow addresses the condition of judgment. Judgment is supported by boundaries, but snow veils those boundaries and destabilizes distinction. The object does not disappear. Yet the perceptual act of separating one thing from another and determining what it is becomes suspended. A gap emerges between what is visible and what can be judged.

Between the mind that tries to accept my father's prognosis and the mind that continues to refuse it, the sense of boundary wavered. The line separating life and death should have been clear. Yet it would not function properly. Standing in a snow field, the boundary between sky and earth was ambiguous. Depth dissolved. What vanished was not the landscape itself, but the ground on which judgment rested. Beneath the snow, shoots rooted in the earth hold their breath. What disappeared was not existence itself, but the criteria through which existence could be confirmed.

Judgment depends on boundaries. Boundaries do not vanish, but they cease to function. Distinction becomes possible, yet confirmation does not follow. Perception remains suspended before conclusion. Certainty dissolves. Existence remains.

For the snow series, I use gelatin silver printing. The silver particles quietly receive light. The image shifts slightly as the viewer moves. Existence continues in silence, yet its appearance is never fixed.

Flower addresses the condition of meaning. A flower may be seen as beauty, or received as season, person, memory, or loss. Its meaning does not settle into one, but emerges each time differently as it connects with the viewer's sensitivity and experience. Meaning does not reside within the object itself, but is generated between object and viewer.

My mother arranged flowers with each season. The flower was a sign indicating the present moment. I was born in October. The scent of osmanthus (a highly fragrant autumn flower deeply rooted in Japanese seasonal consciousness) once announced my birthday. But one year, that scent arrived late. What was lost was not the flower itself. It was the temporal framework through which flowers were read.

Flowers are photographed at night. Clues to species and season are withheld. Seeing does not immediately converge into reading. Flowers have functioned as seasonal signs. But when one cannot identify the flower, the seasonal framework ceases to function. Meaning does not reside within objects. It arises through structures of interpretation. When frameworks waver, objects do not immediately settle. They are visible, yet cannot be identified. The visible does not automatically become legible.

Flowers still bloom. Yet meaning is not fixed. Platinum/palladium printing absorbs light. The image sinks into the interior of the paper, refusing the fixation of meaning.

The moon remains visible. Snow continues to fall. Flowers continue to bloom. Yet what they mean is generated through the relation with the viewer. These three chapters do not describe instability. They construct situations in which the conditions of perception cease to function. Perception continues, but without certainty.

Apples, snow fields, and flowers do not hold complete meaning in themselves. They acquire different meanings each time as they connect with the viewer's memory, emotion, culture, and experience. What this work constructs is not social relations, but epistemological relations. When viewing the work, viewers confront their own structures of visibility, judgment, and meaning. Relations arise not between person and person, but between image and the viewer's sensitivity, memory, and experience.

The subject is not something that indicates meaning. It is an occasion that evokes sensitivity. When the conditions of perception waver, the viewer reconnects with the image through their own memory, emotion, and experience. This work does not present the meaning of the image. It is a work that exposes the connection born between image and viewer.

## Appendix

### Positioning within Contemporary Art

Within contemporary photographic practice, this work marks a turn from photography as representation to photography as a device for epistemological inquiry. Rather than recording objects, it foregrounds the conditions through which objects become visible.

The three conditions—visibility, judgment, and meaning—normally function as transparent presuppositions. By constructing situations in which these conditions cease to function, the work renders them visible.

Just as James Turrell has explored the perception of light and Hiroshi Sugimoto has materialized time, visual art has opened up a domain that interrogates seeing itself. This work focuses specifically on the conditions through which perception is constituted.

---

### Theoretical Background

The theoretical foundation lies in phenomenology. Visibility is not total, boundaries are not always determinate, and meaning is not fixed. These are fundamental characteristics of perception that phenomenology has revealed.

In everyday perception, these incompleteness are normally concealed. We feel we see the world completely and treat boundaries as determinate. The work lifts this concealment by presenting states in which the conditions of perception cease to function.

The paradox that concealment reveals existence—an epistemological stance running through Zeami's "hisureba hana" and Tanizaki Jun'ichirō's In Praise of Shadows—is also present here. This work is not an illustration of Japanese aesthetics but an attempt to connect cultural tradition to contemporary epistemological inquiry.

---

### Resistance to Excessive Visibility

A critical response to contemporary culture's demand for "complete visibility" and "immediate understanding."

In the contemporary moment, there is a strong imperative to see everything, understand immediately, and explain completely. Ambiguity is treated as error, incompleteness as defect.

However, perception does not function this way. Visibility is always partial. Judgment depends on boundaries that are not always fixed. Meaning is not stable. These are not defects of perception but its conditions.

Japanese culture valued the refusal to make everything explicit, the retention of empty space, the beauty that arises through concealment. Yet contemporary culture has inverted this relation. Complete transparency is ethical, immediate understanding is efficient.

This work does not celebrate ambiguity. It asks: when perception is reduced to information processing, when seeing is equated with knowing, what is lost?

The capacity to remain before the uncertain, the capacity to feel without resolving, the capacity to see while suspended. This is the space where imagination and sensitivity operate. The work is an attempt to reclaim that space.

---

### Dialogue with Contemporary Discourse

A critical response to Relational Aesthetics in contemporary art.

Nicolas Bourriaud's Relational Aesthetics foregrounded social relations between artwork and viewer. However, the relations constructed remain primarily within the social dimension.

What this work proposes is the construction of **epistemological relations**. The work engages the conditions of the viewer's perception. When viewing, viewers confront their own structures of visibility, judgment, and meaning. Relations arise between image and the viewer's sensitivity, memory, and experience.

Where Relational Aesthetics problematized "what is seen," this work problematizes "how seeing is constituted."

---

A **critical response to post-photography** in an era of digital excess.

Digital technology infinitely reproduces and accelerates image circulation. Images are expected to provide complete visibility and immediate understanding. Yet what is lost is the experience of seeing over time, of receiving what remains uncertain, of interpretation held in suspension.

Platinum/palladium prints possess durability measured in centuries. This **preservation for posterity** leaves the conditions of perception felt in this era accessible to future generations.

The uniquely broad tonal range of platinum printing **functions as a condition of perception**. Its delicate tonality, the way light sinks into the paper, the subtle gradations—these open the viewer's perception to minute differences.

Hand-printing as unique objects is the **materialization of perception through the viewer's bodily experience**. Viewers "touch the work with their eyes." Taking time to see, changing angles and shifting position, sensing the slight fluctuation of the image—this bodily experience itself exposes the conditions of perception.

---

### Technical Characteristics

The choice of materials and techniques is a methodology for materially constructing the conditions of perception within the viewer's experience.

All works are hand-printed from digital negatives, each produced as a unique object.

**Platinum/palladium prints (Moon, Flower)** absorb light. The image sinks into the paper's interior, directly constructing conditions in which visibility and meaning remain unfixed.

**Gelatin silver prints (Snow)** reflect light. The image changes depending on the viewer's position, materializing conditions in which judgment remains indeterminate through bodily movement.
